Probability distribution

PDF(probability density function)

Definition : $f$ is a continuous function with:

  • $f(x) \ge 0, \forall x$
  • $\int_{-x}^{x} dx = 1$
  • $P(a<x<b) = \int_a^bdx$

  1. Random variables - usually denoted by uppercase letters near the end of our alphabet （e.g. X, Y）.

  2. Particular value - now use lowercase letters, such as x, which correspond to the r.v. X.

if $f$ is a continuous function and

$P(x=a)=P(a\le x \le a)=\int_a^af(x)dx=0,a\in\mathbb{R}$
$$
P(x<=a) \equiv P(x<a)\\
proof:\\
P(x<a \cup x=a)= P(x<a)+P(a)=P(x<a)
$$
Let $X$ be a continuous random variable with associated pdf $f(x)$. Then its expectation and variance are denoted by $\mu$ and $\sigma$

which $\mu = \int_{-\infin}^{+\infin} f(x) dx$ and $\sigma = \int_{-\infin}^{+\infin} (f(x)-x)^2 dx$

Exercise:

suppose $X,Y$ both in $[0,1], f(x,y) =1$, what is the possibility of $P(X>Y)$

Solution:
$$
\begin{align*}
P(X > Y) &= \int_{0}^{1} \int_{0}^{y} 1 , dx , dy \
&= \int_{0}^{1} \left[\int_{0}^{y} 1 , dx\right] , dy \
&= \int_{0}^{1} y , dy \
&= \left[\frac{y^2}{2}\right]_{0}^{1} \
&= \frac{1}{2}
\end{align*}
$$

Important probability Distributions

Binomial:

The binomial distribution is a discrete probability distribution that models the number of successes in a fixed number of independent and identically distributed Bernoulli trials.

The Binomial $r.v.$ counts the total number of successes out of $n$ trials, where $X$ is the number of successes.

• Each trial must be independent of the previous experiment.

• The probability of success must be the same for each trial.

$P(X=x)=\displaystyle\frac {n!} {x!(n-x)!}p^x(1-p)^{n-x}$

$E(X) = np$

$\sigma^2 = V(X) = np(1-p)$

Poisson:

$P(X=x)=\displaystyle e^{-\mu} \frac {\mu^x} {x!}$

$E(X) = \mu$

$V(X) = \mu$

Uniform:

$P(X=x) = \frac 1 {b-a}$

$E(X) = \frac {a+b} 2$

$V(X) = \frac {(b-a)^2} {12}$

Normal (Most important):

$P(X = x) = \displaystyle \frac 1 {\sqrt {2\pi}\sigma}(-\frac 1 2(\frac {x-\mu}\sigma)^2)$

$E(X) = \mu$

$V(X) = \sigma^2$

$Z = \displaystyle \frac {x-u} \sigma$

Exponential

$f(x) = \lambda e ^ {- \lambda x}$

$E(x) = \frac 1 \lambda$

$V(x) = (\frac 1 \lambda)^2$

N.B.: Exponential Distribution focus on the time and Poisson Distribution focus on the count of event.

Central Limit Theory

If the sample size is big enough, it will cluster to a normal distribution, no matter what the original distribution is.
