To have a product between a matrix and a vector, make sure the number of columns of the matrix is equal to the number of components of the vector

A diagonal matrix is a special type of square matrix in which all the entries outside the main diagonal are zero. The main diagonal runs from the top-left corner to the bottom-right corner of the matrix, and it contains all the non-zero elements of the matrix. In mathematical notation, if you have a square matrix A with elements $a_{ij}$, where i and j are row and column indices, a diagonal matrix can be represented as:
$$
A = \begin{bmatrix}
a_{11} & 0 & 0 & \ldots & 0 \\
0 & a_{22} & 0 & \ldots & 0 \\
0 & 0 & a_{33} & \ldots & 0 \\
\vdots & \vdots & \vdots & \ddots & \vdots \\
0 & 0 & 0 & \ldots & a_{nn} \\
\end{bmatrix}
$$
Normalized vector: Normalizing a vector means scaling it to have a length (magnitude) of 1 while preserving its direction.

For example:
$$
\text{for}\quad u = (1,3) \
|u| = (\frac 1 {\sqrt {10}},\frac 3 {\sqrt {10}})
$$

Colinear vectors

2 vectors ($\vec u,\vec v$) are collinear if and only if $\exists k \in \mathbb{R} | \vec u = k\vec v \quad \text{or}\quad \vec v =k\vec u$. In the graph, $\vec u, \vec v$ are colinear, they are parallel.

$\vec 0$ is colinear to each vector.

Orthogonal vectors

$\vec u,\vec v $ are orthogonal vectors if $\vec u \cdot \vec v =0$

Product of Matrix

for $m \in M_{\displaystyle{m,n}^{(\mathbb{R})}}$ and $n \in M_{\displaystyle{n,p}^{(\mathbb{R})}}$, $m \cdot n = C_{\displaystyle{m,p}^{(\mathbb{R})}}$

$\det(A) \neq 0 \Leftrightarrow A\cdot A^{-1}=A^{-1} \cdot A = I(\text{Indentity matrix})$ and $I\cdot A =A\cdot I = A$

(Reminder:

$$I_2 = \begin{bmatrix} 1&0\0&1 \end{bmatrix}$$ and $$I_3 = \begin{bmatrix}1&0&0\0&1&0\0&0&1\end{bmatrix}$$)

In general, $A \cdot B \neq B \cdot A$

Determinant of Matrix

In the $R^2$, the determinant is the area, in the $R^3 \quad\text{or}\quad R^n$, it is the volume.

for $$A = \begin{bmatrix} a b \ c d\end{bmatrix}$$, $\det(A) = ad-bc$

for the higher dimention matrix, such as

$$\begin{align*}
\text{det}(A) &= \begin{vmatrix}
a & b & c & d \\
e & f & g & h \\
i & j & k & l \\
m & n & o & p
\end{vmatrix} \\
&= a \begin{vmatrix}
f & g & h \\
j & k & l \\
n & o & p
\end{vmatrix} - b \begin{vmatrix}
e & g & h \\
i & k & l \\
m & o & p
\end{vmatrix} + c \begin{vmatrix}
e & f & h \\
i & j & l \\
m & n & p
\end{vmatrix} - d \begin{vmatrix}
e & f & g \\
i & j & k \\
m & n & o
\end{vmatrix}
\end{align*}$$

If A has a row that is all zeros, then $\det A = 0$.

$\det AB = (\det A)(\det B)$

If there are 2 same rows in the matrix A, the $\det A = 0$

Inverse of Matrix

$ A^{-1} = (adj A)/(\det A)$

For a $3*3$ Matrix

  1. Check if the Matrix is Invertible (Non-Singular)
  2. Calculate the minor of the origin
  3. Find the cofactor
  4. Find the adjoint
  5. Calute the inverse

Eg:
$$
\begin{align*}
&\text{find the inverse matrix of A}\\
&A = \begin{vmatrix}
2&-1&3\
0&5&2\
1&-1&-2\
\end{vmatrix}\\

&\text{1. Check the determinate:}\
&\det(A) = -33\\
&\text{2. Find the minor:}\
&M_{11} = \begin{vmatrix}
5&2\
-1&-2
\end{vmatrix} = 5*(-2)-2*(-1) = -8\
&M_{12} = -2\
&M_{13} = -5\
&M_{21} = 5\
&M_{22} = -7\
&M_{23} = -1\
&M_{31} = -17\
&M_{32} = 4\
&M_{33} = 10\
&\text{So, the minor of A is:}
B = \begin{vmatrix}
-8&-2&-5\
5&-7&-1\
-17&4&10\
\end{vmatrix}\\
&\text{3.the cofactor:}
C= \begin{vmatrix}
-8&2&-5\
-5&-7&1\
-17&-4&10\
\end{vmatrix}\\
&\text{4. find the adjoint:}\
&adjB = C^T = \begin{vmatrix}
-8&-5&-17\
2&-7&-4\
-5&1&10\
\end{vmatrix}\\
&\text{5. Calculate the inverse:}\
&A^{-1} = -1/33 * \begin{vmatrix}
-8&2&-5\
-5&-7&1\
-17&-4&10\
\end{vmatrix}\\
\end{align*}
$$

Eigenvalues & Eigenvectors of a quare matrix

$Au = \lambda u$, $\lambda$ is the eigenvalue of $A$ and u is the eigenvector of $A$

How to calculate?

  • Solve the characterstic polynomial to find the eigenvalues: $\det(A-\lambda I) =0$

  • For every $\lambda_i$ (Eigenvalues), solve $Au=\lambda_iu$ to find the eigenvendors

$$
\begin{aligned}
Au &= \lambda u \\
Au-\lambda Iu &= 0\\
(A-\lambda I)u &= 0\\
\det (A-\lambda I)&=0
\end{aligned}
$$
