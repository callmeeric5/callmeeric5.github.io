Integration by Substitution#

Let $F$ and $g$ be diﬀerentiable functions, where the range of $g$ is an interval $I$ contained in the domain of $F$. Then

$\displaystyle \int F’(g(x))g’(x) dx =F(g(x))+C $

If $u = g(x)$, then $du = g’(x)dx$ and

$\displaystyle\int F’(g(x))g’(x) dx = \displaystyle\int F’(u)du = F(u)+C = F(g(x))+C$

Substitution With A Linear Function

Consider $\int F ′ (ax + b) dx$, where $a$ and $b$ are constants. Letting $u = ax + b$ gives $du = a · dx$, leading to the result

$\displaystyle\int F’(ax+b) d = \displaystyle\frac 1 a F(ax+b) + C $

Antiderivatives of Trigonometric Functions#

$$ \begin{align*} &\int sinx dx = -cosx+C \quad &\int cscx dx = -ln|cscx+cotx|+C\\\\ &\int cosx dx = sinx+C \quad &\int secx dx = ln|secx + tanx|+C \\\\ & \int tanx dx = -ln|cosx|+C \quad &\int cotx dx = ln|sinx|+C \end{align*} $$

Integration by Parts#

Let $u$ and $v$ be diﬀerentiable functions of $x$ on an interval $I$ containing $a$ and $b$. Then

$$ \begin{align*} \int u dv = uv - \int vdu \\\\ \text{and} \\\\ \int_a^b u dv = uv \Big|_a^b - \int_{x=a}^{x=b} vdu \end{align*} $$

TIPS: Using LIATE notation to choose $u$

L = Logarithmic, I = Inverse Trig., A = Algebraic (polynomials), T = Trigonometric, and E = Exponential.

Partial Fraction Decomposition#

Let $\displaystyle\frac {p(x)} {q(x)}$be a rational function, where the degree of $p$ is less than the degree of $q$.

Linear Terms: Let $(x−a)$ divide $q(x)$, where $(x−a)^n$ is the highest power of $(x−a)$ that divides $q(x)$. Then the decomposition of $\displaystyle\frac {p(x)} {q(x)}$ will contain the sum

$\displaystyle\frac {A_1} {(x-a)}+\displaystyle\frac {A_2} {(x-a)^2}+\cdot\cdot\cdot+\displaystyle\frac {A_1} {(x-a)^n}$

Quadratic Terms: Let $x ^2+ bx + c$ divide $q(x)$, where $(x ^2+ bx + c)^n$ is the highest power of $x ^2 + bx + c$ that divides $q(x)$. Then the decomposition of $\displaystyle\frac {q(x)} {p(x)}$ will contain the sum

$\displaystyle\frac {B_1 x+C_1} {(x ^2+ bx + c)}+\displaystyle\frac {B_1 x+C_1} {(x ^2+ bx + c)^2}+\cdot\cdot\cdot+\displaystyle\frac {B_1 x+C_1} {(x ^2+ bx + c)^n}$

To ﬁnd the coeﬃcients $A_i , B_i \text{and} C_i$ :

  1. Multiply all fractions by $q(x)$, clearing the denominators. Collect like terms.

  2. Equate the resulting coeﬃcients of the powers of $x$ and solve the resulting system of linear equations.

L’Hôpital’s Rule#

  1. Let$\displaystyle\lim_{x \to c} f(x) = 0$ and$\displaystyle\lim_{x \to c} g(x) = 0$ where $f$ and $g$ are diﬀerentiable functions on an open interval $I$ containing $c$, and $g ′ (x) \neq 0$ on $I$ except possible at $c$. Then

​ $\displaystyle\lim_{x \to c} \frac {f(x)}{g(x)} = \displaystyle\lim_{x \to c} \frac {f’(x)}{g’(x)}$

  1. Let$\displaystyle\lim_{x \to a} f(x) = 0$ and$\displaystyle\lim_{x \to a} g(x) = 0$ where $f$ and $g$ are diﬀerentiable functions on an open interval $I$ containing $a$, and $g ′ (x) \neq 0$ on $I$ except possible at $a$. Then

​ $\displaystyle\lim_{x \to a} \frac {f(x)}{g(x)} = \displaystyle\lim_{x \to a} \frac {f’(x)}{g’(x)}$

  1. Let $f$ and $g$ be diﬀerentiable functions on the open interval $(a, ∞)$ for some value $a$, where $g ′ (x) \neq 0$ on $(a, ∞)$ and $\displaystyle\lim_{x \to \infty} f(x)/g(x)$ returns either $0/0$ or $∞/∞$. Then

​ $\displaystyle\lim_{x \to \infty} \frac {f(x)}{g(x)} = \displaystyle\lim_{x \to \infty} \frac {f’(x)}{g’(x)}$

NB: the function’s form is supposed to be $\frac 0 0$ or $\frac \infty \infty$

Evaluating Limits Involving Indeterminate Forms $ 0^0$ , $ 1^∞$ and $∞^0$

If $\displaystyle \lim_{x \to c} ln ( f(x) ) = L$, then $\displaystyle \lim_{x \to c} f(x) = \displaystyle \lim_{x \to c} e^{lnf(x)}=e^L$ .

Improper Integrals#

  1. Let f be a continuous function on $[a, ∞)$. Deﬁne

​ $\displaystyle \int_a^\infty f(x)dx \quad\text{to be}\quad \displaystyle\lim_{b\to\infty}\int_a^b f(x)dx$

  1. Let f be a continuous function on $(−∞, b]$. Deﬁne

​ $\displaystyle \int_{-\infty}^b f(x)dx \quad\text{to be}\quad \displaystyle\lim_{a\to-\infty}\int_a^b f(x)dx$

  1. Let f be a continuous function on $(−∞, ∞)$. Let $c$ be any real number; deﬁne

​ $\displaystyle \int_{-\infty}^\infty f(x)dx \quad\text{to be}\quad \displaystyle\lim_{a\to-\infty}\int_a^b f(x)dx+\displaystyle\lim_{b\to\infty}\int_a^b f(x)dx$

An improper integral is said to converge if its corresponding limit exists; otherwise, it diverges. The improper integral in part 3 converges if and only if both of its limits exist.

Improper Integration with Inﬁnite Range#

Let $f(x)$ be a continuous function on $[a, b]$ except at $c, a ≤ c ≤ b$, where $x = c$ is a vertical asymptote of $f$. Deﬁne

$\displaystyle \int_{a}^b f(x)dx = \displaystyle\lim_{t\to c-}\int_a^t f(x)dx+\displaystyle\lim_{t\to c+}\int_t^b f(x)dx$

Convergence of Improper Integrals $\displaystyle \int_{1}^\infty \frac 1 {x^p}dx$ and $\displaystyle \int_{0}^1 \frac 1 {x^p}dx$

  1. The improper integral $\displaystyle \int_{1}^\infty \frac 1 {x^p}dx$ converges when p > 1 and diverges when p ≤ 1.

  2. The improper integral$\displaystyle \int_{0}^1 \frac 1 {x^p}dx$ dx converges when p < 1 and diverges when p ≥ 1.

Direct Comparison Test for Improper Integrals

Let $f$ and $g$ be continuous on $[a, ∞)$ where 0 ≤ f(x) ≤ g(x) for all x in $[a, ∞)$.

  1. If $\displaystyle \int_{a}^\infty g(x)dx$ converges, then $\displaystyle \int_{a}^\infty f(x)dx$

  2. If $\displaystyle \int_{a}^\infty g(x)dx$ diverges, then$\displaystyle \int_{a}^\infty g(x)dx$

Limit Comparison Test for Improper Integrals

Let f and g be continuous functions on $[a, ∞)$ where $f(x) > 0$ and $g(x) > 0$ for all x. If

$\displaystyle \lim_{x\to\infty}\frac {f(x)} {g(x)}=L, 0<L<\infty$

Then $\displaystyle \int_{a}^\infty f(x) dx$ and $\displaystyle \int_{a}^\infty g(x) dx$

either both converge or both diverge.
