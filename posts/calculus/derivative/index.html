Definition#

Instantaneous rate of change at the point of the function. Presentation in Math,$f’(c)=\lim\limits_{h\to0}\dfrac {f(c+h)-f(c)} h$, the tangent line of $f$ at $c$ will go through $(c,f(c))$, the equation is $l(x)=f’(c)(x-c)+f(c)$.

If we draw the derivatives of each point in the graph, we can get a new function called the derivative function, the equation is $f’(x)=\lim\limits_{h\to0}\dfrac {f(x+h)-f(x)} h$, It also writen as $f’(x)=y’=\dfrac {dy} {dx}=\dfrac {df} {dx}=\dfrac d {dx}(y)=\dfrac d {dx}(f)$

Common Derivative Functions#

$$ \begin{aligned} &\frac d {dx}(c) = 0 \qquad &\frac d {dx}(ax) = a\\\\ &\frac d {dx}(x^n) = nx^{n-1} \qquad &\frac d {dx}(e^x) = e^x\\\\ &\frac d {dx}(sinx) = cosx \qquad &\frac d {dx}(cosx) = -sinx\\\\ &\frac d {dx}(tanx) = sec^2x \qquad &\frac d {dx}(lnx) = \frac 1 x\\\\ &\frac d {dx}(a^x) = a^xlna \qquad &\frac d{dx}(x^x) = x^x(lnx+1) \end{aligned} $$

Properties of Derivatives#

$$ \begin{aligned} &\text{Sum Rule:} \\\\ &\frac d {dx}(f(x)\pm g(x)) = \frac d {dx}(f(x))\pm \frac d {dx}(g(x))=f'(x) \pm g'(x)\\\\ &\text{Constant Multiple Rule:} \\\\ &\frac d {dx}(cf(x)) = c\frac d {dx}(f(x)) = cf'(x) \\\\ &\text{Product Rule:}\\\\ &\frac d {dx}(f(x)g(x)) = f'(x)g(x)+f(x)g'(x)\\\\ &\text{Quotient Rule:} \\\\ &\frac d {dx}(\frac {f(x)} {g(x)}) = \frac {f'(x)g(x)-f(x)g'(x)} {g^2(x)} \end{aligned} $$

Chain Rule#

$f\circ g=f(g(x))$ means the composition of $f(x)$ and $g(x)$, the range of $g(x)$ is the interval of $f(x)$.

If we want to get the derivative of $y=f(g(x))$, use the chain rule:

$y’=f’(g(x))\cdot g’(x)$, we often use $u=g(x)$ to present it:

$$ \begin{aligned} &\frac d {dy} u^n = n(u)^{n-1}\cdot u' \qquad &\frac d {dy} cosu = -sinu\cdot u'\\\\ &\frac d {dy} e^{u} = e^u\cdot u'\qquad &\frac d {dy} tanu = sec^2u\cdot u'\\\\ &\frac d {dy} sinu = cosu\cdot u'\\\\ \end{aligned} $$

Implicit Diﬀerentiation#

We have known $\dfrac d {dx} f(g(x)) = f’(g(x))\cdot g’(x)$, let $y=g(x)$, we can write as $\dfrac d {dx} f(y) = f’(y)\cdot y’=f’(y) \dfrac {dy} {dx}$

For instance, how to get $y’$ of the function $x^2+y^2=1$?

$$ \begin{aligned} &(x^2+y^2)'=1'\\\\ &2x+2y\cdot y'=0 \qquad.......\text{Let $f(y)=y^2$}\\\\ &y'=-\frac y x \end{aligned} $$

Inverse Function#

Let $f(x)$and $g(x)$ be two one-to-one functions. If $(f\circ g)(x)=f(g(x))=x$ then we say that $f(x)$ and $g(x)$ are inverses of each other. We denote $g(x)$ (the inverse of $f(x)$ by $g(x)=f^{−1}(x)$.

Attention: $f^{-1}(x) \neq \dfrac 1 {f(x)}$, $f^{-1}$ is a symbol not a exponentiation.

Let $y=g(x)$ and we have known $f(y)=x$ so that

$$ \begin{aligned} \dfrac d {dx} f(y) &= \dfrac d {dx} x\\\\ f'(y)\cdot y' &= 1\\\\ y' &=\frac 1 {f'(y)}\\\\ y'&=(f^{-1})'(x)=g'(x)=\frac 1 {f'(g(x))} \end{aligned} $$

Common Inverse Derivative Functions#

$$ \begin{aligned} &\frac d {dx}(sin^{-1}x) = \dfrac 1 {\sqrt {1-x^2}} \qquad &\frac d {dx}(cos^{-1}x) = -\dfrac 1 {\sqrt {1-x^2}}\\\\ &\frac d {dx}(tan^{-1}x) = \dfrac 1 {1+x^2} \qquad \end{aligned} $$

Extreme Value#

If $f$ is continuous in a closed interval $I=[a,b]$, $f$ has both maximum and minimum values called extreme values.

We can find the extreme value by derivative. When $f’(c)=0 \ or\ f’(c)\ is \ not \ definied$, $c$ is the critical value of the function. We will get extreme value from $a$ or $b$ or the critical value $c$.

Mean value theorem#

If $f$ is continuous in a closed interval $I=[a,b]$, and differentiable on an open interval $I$, it exists a value $c$ in interval $I$(a<c<b), such that $f’(c) =\dfrac {f(b)-f(a)} {b-a}$. Also, if $f(a) = f(b)$, there is some c in $(a,b)$, such that $f’(c) = 0$

Increasing or Decreasing#

Let $f$ be a continuous function on $[a,b]$ and differentiable on $(a,b)$:

  • $f’(c)>0$ for all $c \ in \ (a,b)$, then $f$ is increasing on $[a,b]$
  • $f’(c)<0$ for all $c \ in \ (a,b)$, then $f$ is decreasing on $[a,b]$
  • $f’(c)=0$ for all $c \ in \ (a,b)$, then $f$ is constant on $[a,b]$

We can find critical values of $f$, divide $I$ into subintervals, then pick any values in the subinterval and find its derivative.

Concavity#

Suppose $f$ is differentiable on an interval I. The graph of f is concave up if $f’’> 0 \ on \ I$, and is concave down if $f’’< 0\ on\ I$. If $(c, f(c))$ is a point of inﬂection on the graph of $f$, then either $f’’(c) = 0$ or $f’’$ is not deﬁned at $c$.

  • If $f’’(c) > 0$, then $f$ has a local minimum at $(c, f(c))$.
  • If $f’’ (c) < 0$, then $f$ has a local maximum at $(c, f(c))$.

Newton’s Method#

Let $f$ be a diﬀerentiable function on an interval I with a root in I, $ x_{n+1} = x_n - \dfrac {f(x)} {f’(x)}$ (Because the tangent line of $f$ will meet the x-axis). Sometimes, it is hard to use Newtons’s Method because of the uncertainty of $f(x_0)$

Related Rates#

When two quantities are related by an equation, knowing the value of one quantity can determine the value of the other.

Differentials#

Let $y = f(x)$ be diﬀerentiable. The diﬀerential of $x$, denoted $dx$, is any nonzero real number (usually taken to be a small number). The diﬀerential of $y$, denoted $dy$, is $ dy = f’(x)dx$

Numerical Integration#

Let f be a continuous function on $[a, b]$, let $n$ be a positive integer, and let $\Delta x = \displaystyle\frac {b-a} n$.Set $x_1 = a, x_x= a + \Delta x, . . ., x_i = a + (i − 1)\Delta x, x_n+1 = b$. Consider $\displaystyle\int_a^b f(x)dx$

$$ \begin{align*} &\text {Left Hand Rule}: \displaystyle\int_a^b f(x)dx \approx \Delta x[f(x_1)+f(x_2)+...+f(x_n)]\\\\ &\text {Right Hand Rule}: \displaystyle\int_a^b f(x)dx \approx \Delta x[f(x_2)+f(x_3)+...+f(x_{n+1})]\\\\ &\text {Trapezoidal Rule}:\displaystyle\int_a^b f(x)dx \approx \frac {\Delta x} 2 [f(x_1)+2f(x_2)+...+2f(x_n)+f(x_{n+1})]\\\\ &\text {Simpson’s Rule}:\displaystyle\int_a^b f(x)dx \approx \frac {\Delta x} 3 [f(x_1)+4f(x_2)+2f(x_3)...+2f(x_{n-1})+4f(x_n)+f(x_{n+1})]\text{(n is even)}\\\\ \end{align*} $$
