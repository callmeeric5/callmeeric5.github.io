Sequence#

A sequence is a function $a(n)$ whose domain is $\mathbb{N}$. The range of sequence is the set of all distinct values of $a(n)$.

The terms of a sequence are the values $a(1), a(2), …,a(n)$ which are usually denoted with subscripts as $a_1 , a_2 , ….a_n$

A sequence $a(n)$ is often denoted as ${a_n}$ .

Limit of a Sequence#

Let ${a_n}$ be a sequence and let $L$ be a real number. Given any $ε > 0$, if an m can be found such that $| a_n − L | < ε$ for all $n > m$, then we say the limit of ${ a_n }$ , as n approaches inﬁnity, is $L$, denoted $\displaystyle\lim_{n\to\infty}a_n = L$

If $\displaystyle\lim_{n\to\infty}a_n = L$exists, we say the sequence converges; otherwise, the sequence diverges.

Let ${ a_n }$ be a sequence and let $f(x)$ be a function whose domain contains the positive real numbers where $f(n) = a_n$ for all $n$ in $\mathbb{N}$.

if $\displaystyle\lim_{x\to\infty}f(x) = L$, then $\displaystyle\lim_{n\to\infty}a_n = L$

Absolute Value Theorem

Let ${ a_n }$ be a sequence. If $\displaystyle\lim_{n\to\infty}|a_n| = 0$, then$\displaystyle\lim_{n\to\infty}a_n = 0$

Properties of the Limits of Sequences

Let ${ a_n }$ and ${ b_n}$ be sequences such that $\displaystyle\lim_{n\to\infty}a_n = L$ , and $\displaystyle\lim_{n\to\infty}b_n = K$ and let c be a real number.

$$ \begin{align*} &\displaystyle\lim_{n\to\infty}(a_n \pm b_n)= L \pm K &\displaystyle\lim_{n\to\infty}(a_n/b_n) = L/K (l\neq 0)\\\\ &\displaystyle\lim_{n\to\infty}a_n \cdot b_n = L \cdot K &\displaystyle\lim_{n\to\infty}a_n \cdot c = c \cdot L \end{align*} $$

Bounded and Unbounded Sequences

A sequence ${ a_n }$ is said to be bounded if there exist real numbers $m$ and $M$ such that $m < a_n < M$ for all $n$ in $\mathbb{N}$.

A sequence ${ a_n }$ is said to be unbounded if it is not bounded.

A sequence ${ a_n }$ is said to be bounded above if there exists an $M$ such that $a_n < M$ for all $n$ in $\mathbb{N}$; it is bounded below if there exists an $m$ such that $m < a_n$ for all $n$ in $\mathbb{N}$.

Geometric Series Test

Consider the geometric series $\displaystyle\sum_{n=0}^\infty r^n$

The $n^{th}$ partial sum is: $S_n = \displaystyle \frac {1-r^{n+1} }{1-r} (r \neq 1) $.

The series converges if, and only if, $| r | < 1$. When $| r | < 1$, $\displaystyle\sum_{n=0}^\infty r^n = \frac 1 {1-r}$

p–Series Test

A general p–series $\displaystyle\sum_{n=1}^\infty \frac 1 {(an + b)^p}$ will converge if, and only if $p > 1$.

Important Series#

  1. Geometric series:

The formula for a geometric series is given by:

$$\sum_{n=0}^{\infty} ar^n = \frac{a}{1-r}, \quad \text{if } |r| < 1$$
  1. Harmonic series:

The formula for the harmonic series is given by:

$$\sum_{n=1}^{\infty} \frac{1}{n} = \infty$$
  1. Alternating series:

The formula for an alternating series is given by:

$$\sum_{n=0}^{\infty} (-1)^n a_n = a_0 - a_1 + a_2 - a_3 + \cdots$$

If the alternating series satisfies the conditions of the Alternating Series Test, then the series converges to a value between the first two partial sums.

  1. Riemann zeta function:

The Riemann zeta function is given by:

$$\zeta(s) = \sum_{n=1}^{\infty} \frac{1}{n^s}$$

The Riemann zeta function converges for all values of $s$ with real part greater than $1$. In particular, $\zeta(2) = \frac{\pi^2}{6}$.

  1. Taylor series:

The Taylor seriesof a function $f(x)$ centered at $a$ is given by:

$$f(x) = \sum_{n=0}^{\infty} \frac{f^{(n)}(a)}{n!}(x-a)^n$$

The value of the Taylor series at $x=a$ is equal to $f(a)$.

  1. Fourier series:

The Fourier series of a function $f(x)$ with period $2\pi$ is given by:

$$f(x) = \frac{a_0}{2} + \sum_{n=1}^{\infty} \left(a_n \cos(nx) + b_n \sin(nx)\right)$$

where

$$a_0 = \frac{1}{\pi} \int_{-\pi}^{\pi} f(x) dx$$ $$a_n = \frac{1}{\pi} \int_{-\pi}^{\pi} f(x) \cos(nx) dx$$ $$b_n = \frac{1}{\pi} \int_{-\pi}^{\pi} f(x) \sin(nx) dx$$
  1. Power series:

A power series centered at $a$ is given by:

$$\sum_{n=0}^{\infty} c_n (x-a)^n$$

The value of the power series at $x=a$ is equal to $c_0$.

  1. Binomial series:

The binomial series is given by:

$$(1+x)^n = \sum_{k=0}^{n} \binom{n}{k} x^k$$

where $\binom{n}{k}$ is the binomial coefficient. In particular,

$$(1+x)^{-1} = \sum_{n=0}^{\infty} (-1)^n x^n, \quad \text{if } |x| < 1$$ $$(1+x)^{1/2} = \sum_{n=0}^{\infty} \binom{1/2}{n} x^n, \quad \text{if } |x| < 1$$

where $\binom{1/2}{n} = \frac{(-1)^n (2n-1)!!}{2^n n!}$ is the generalized binomial coefficient.

  1. Exponential series:

The exponential series is given by:

$$e^x = \sum_{n=0}^{\infty} \frac{x^n}{n!}$$

In particular,

$$e = \sum_{n=0}^{\infty} \frac{1}{n!}$$
  1. Trigonometric series:

A trigonometric series is given by:

$$a_0 + \sum_{n=1}^{\infty} \left(a_n \cos(nx) + b_n \sin(nx)\right)$$

where $a_n$ and $b_n$ are constants. The value of the trigonometric series depends on the specific values of $a_n$ and $b_n$, and the function being approximated.

Taylor’s Theorem

Let $f(x)$ be a function that is $n+1$ times differentiable on an interval containing $a$. Then, for any $x$ in that interval, there exists a number $c$ between $x$ and $a$ such that

$$f(x) = \sum_{k=0}^n \frac{f^{(k)}(a)}{k!}(x-a)^k + R_n(x)$$

where $f^{(k)}(a)$ denotes the $k$th derivative of $f$ evaluated at $a$, and $R_n(x)$ is the remainder term, given by

$$R_n(x) = \frac{f^{(n+1)}(c)}{(n+1)!}(x-a)^{n+1}$$

The remainder term represents the difference between the actual value of $f(x)$ and its Taylor series approximation. The size of the remainder term depends on the value of $f^{(n+1)}(c)$, which can be difficult to estimate in practice.

The special case $n=0$ gives us the first order approximation of $f(x)$ around $a$, which is the linearization of $f$ at $a$:

$$f(x) \approx f(a) + f'(a)(x-a)$$

This is the equation for the tangent line to the graph of $f$ at $(a,f(a))$.

Taylor and Maclaurin Series

Let f(x) have derivatives of all orders at $x = c$.

  1. The Taylor Series of $f(x)$, centred at $c$ is
$$ \sum_{n=0}^\infty \frac {f^{(n)}(c)} {n!}(x-c)^n $$
  1. Setting c = 0 gives the Maclaurin Series of $f(x)$:
$$ \sum_{n=0}^\infty \frac {f^{(n)}(0)} {n!}(x-c)^n $$

NB. How to understander Taylor’s Theorm?

The essence of Taylor’s Theorem lies in its remarkable concept: any curve can be approximated by a straight line by carefully selecting two points that are extremely close to each other on the graph. This fundamental idea, rooted in the principles of Calculus, allows us to express a linear function at a specific point $x_0$ simply as $f_1 = f’(x_0)x + b$. By applying this process repeatedly to two neighbouring points on $f_1$, we can derive another line, denoted as $f_2 = f’_1(x_0)x + c.$ Continuing this iterative approach leads us to a fascinating observation—the newly formed graph bears a striking resemblance to the original one that we are already familiar with.

Important Taylor Series Expansions#

  1. $\displaystyle e^x = \sum_{n=0}^{\infty} \frac{x^n}{n!}$, for $-\infty < x < \infty$
  2. $\displaystyle \sin x = \sum_{n=0}^{\infty} \frac{(-1)^n}{(2n+1)!} x^{2n+1}$, for $-\infty < x < \infty$
  3. $\displaystyle \cos x = \sum_{n=0}^{\infty} \frac{(-1)^n}{(2n)!} x^{2n}$, for $-\infty < x < \infty$
  4. $\displaystyle \frac{1}{1-x} = \sum_{n=0}^{\infty} x^n$, for $-1 < x < 1$
  5. $\displaystyle \ln(1+x) = \sum_{n=1}^{\infty} \frac{(-1)^{n+1}}{n}x^n$, for $-1 < x \leq 1$
  6. $\displaystyle \arctan x = \sum_{n=0}^{\infty} \frac{(-1)^n}{2n+1} x^{2n+1}$, for $-1 \leq x \leq 1$
  7. $\displaystyle \frac{1}{1+x^2} = \sum_{n=0}^{\infty} (-1)^n x^{2n}$, for $-1 \leq x \leq 1$
  8. $\displaystyle \sqrt{1+x} = \sum_{n=0}^{\infty} \binom{1/2}{n} x^n$, for $-1 < x \leq 1$

Algebra of Power Series#

Let $f(x) =\displaystyle \sum_{n=0}^{\infty} a_nx^n$ and $g(x) =\displaystyle \sum_{n=0}^{\infty} b_nx^n$ converge absolutely for $| x | < R$, and let $h(x)$ be continuous.

  1. $f(x) \pm g(x) = \sum_{n=0}^{\infty} (a_n \pm b_n)x^n \qquad\text{for} |x|<R$
  2. $f(x) \cdot g(x) = (\sum_{n=0}^{\infty} a_nx^n)(\sum_{n=0}^{\infty} b_nx^n)=\sum_{n=0}^{\infty} (a_0b_n+a_1b_{n-1}+…+a_nb_0)x^n \quad \text{for} |x|<R $
  3. $f(h(x))=\sum_{n=0}^{\infty} (a_n)(h(x))^n \quad\text{for} |h(x)|<R $
