Three equivalent ways of thinking about a linear system:#

  1. As a system of equations:
$$ \begin{cases} 2x + 3x_2 − 2x_3 = 7\\\\ x_1− x_2− 3x_3= 5 \end{cases} $$
  1. As an augmented matrix:
$$ \left( \begin{array}{ccc} 2 & 3 & -2 \\\\ 1 & -1 & -3 \end{array} \middle| \begin{array}{c} 7 \\\\ 5 \end{array} \right) $$
  1. As a vector equation $(x_1v_1 + x_2v_2 + ··· + x_nv_n = b)$:
$$ x_1\begin{pmatrix}2\\\\1\end{pmatrix}+x_2\begin{pmatrix}3\\\\-1\end{pmatrix}+x_3\begin{pmatrix}-2\\\\-3\end{pmatrix}=\begin{pmatrix}7\\\\5\end{pmatrix} $$

Span#

Given a set of vectors ${v_1, v_2, \ldots, v_n}$ in a vector space $V$, the span of that set, denoted by $\text{Span}{v_1, v_2, \ldots, v_n}$, is the set of all linear combinations of those vectors:

$$ \text{Span}\lbrace v_1, v_2, \ldots, v_n\rbrace = \lbrace a_1v_1 + a_2v_2 + \ldots + a_nv_n \mid a_1, a_2, \ldots, a_n \in \mathbb{R}\rbrace $$

In other words, the span is the set of all possible vectors that can be written as a linear combination of the given vectors. Geometrically, the span of a set of vectors is the subspace of the vector space $V$ that is generated by those vectors.

Matrix Equations#

Let $A$ be an $m × n$ matrix in with columns $a_{m1}, a_{m2},…, a_{mn}$:

$$ A = \begin{bmatrix} a_{11} & a_{12} & \ldots & a_{1n} \\\\ a_{21} & a_{22} & \ldots & a_{2n} \\\\ \vdots & \vdots & \ddots & \vdots \\\\ a_{m1} & a_{m2} & \ldots & a_{mn} \\\\ \end{bmatrix} $$

Let $c$ be a scalar and let $u, v$ be vectors in $R^n$, then:

• $A(u + v) = Au + Av$

• $A(cu) = cAu$

The following are equivalent:

  1. $Ax = b$ has a solution for all $b$ in $R^m$ .
  2. The span of the columns of $A$ is all of $R^m$ .
  3. $A$ has a pivot position in every row.

Solution Sets#

If $Ax = b$ is consistent, the set of solutions is obtained by taking one particular solution $p$ of $Ax = b$, and adding all solutions of $Ax = 0$. In particular, if $Ax = b$ is consistent, the solution set is a translation of a span.

The solution set: for ﬁxed $b$, this is the set of all $x$ such that $Ax = b$.

  • This is a span if $b = 0$, and it is a translation of a span if $b = 0$ (and $Ax = b$ is consistent).

  • It is a subset of $R^n$ .

  • It is computed by solving a system of equations, usually by row reducing and ﬁnding the parametric vector form.

The span of the columns of A: this is the set of all $b$ such that $Ax = b$ is consistent.

  • This is always a span.

  • It is a subset of $R^m$ .

  • It is not computed by solving a system of equations, row reduction plays no role.

Linear Independence#

${v_1, v_2,…, v_k}$ is linearly dependent if there exist numbers $x_1, x_2 ,…, x_k$ , not all equal to zero, such that

, $x_1v_1 + x_2v_2 + ··· + x_k v_k = 0.$

NB: A wide matrix (a matrix with more columns than rows) has linearly dependent columns.

  1. Two vectors are linearly dependent if and only if they are collinear, i.e., one is a scalar multiple of the other.
  2. Any set containing the zero vector is linearly dependent.
  3. If a subset of $\lbrace v_1 , v_2 ,…, v_k\rbrace$ is linearly dependent, then ${v_1 , v_2 ,…, v_k }$ is linearly dependent as well.

Criteria for Linear Independence#

  1. A set of vectors $\lbrace v_1, v_2,…, v_k\rbrace$ is linearly dependent if and only if one of the vectors is in the span of the other ones. Any such vector may be removed without affecting the span.

  2. A set of vectors $\lbrace v_1, v_2,…, v_k\rbrace$ is linearly independent if and only if, for every $j$, the vector $v_j$ is not in Span $\lbrace v_1, v_2 ,…v_{j-1}\rbrace$, it can be rephrased as if make a set of vectors by adding one vector at a time, and if the span got bigger every time you added a vector, then your set is linearly independent.

Subspaces#

A subset of $R^n$ is any collection of points of $R^n$.

A subspace of $R^n$ is a subset $V$ of $R^n$ satisfying:

  1. Non-emptiness: The zero vector is in $V$.

  2. Closure under addition: If $u$ and $v$ are in $V$, then $u + v$ is also in $V$.

  3. Closure under scalar multiplication: If $v$ is in $V$ and $c$ is in $R$, then $cv$ is also in $V.$

Conclusion: A subspace is a subset that happens to satisfy the three additional deﬁning properties.

Common Types of Subspaces#

(Spans are Subspaces and Subspaces are Spans). If $v_1, v_2,…, v_p$ are any vectors in $R^n$ , then $\text{Span}\lbrace v_1 , v_2 ,…, v_p\rbrace $is a subspace of $R^n$ . Moreover, any subspace of $R^n$ can be written as a span of a set of $p$ linearly independent vectors in $R^n$ for $p ≤ n$.

Basis of a Subspace#

Let $V$ be a subspace of $R^n$. A basis of $V$ is a set of vectors $\lbrace v_1 , v_2 ,…, v_n \rbrace$ in $V$ such that:

  1. $V = \text{Span}\lbrace v_1 , v_2 ,…, v_n \rbrace$, and

  2. the set $\lbrace v_1 , v_2 ,…, v_n \rbrace$ is linearly independent.

The number of vectors in any basis of $V$ is called the dimension of $V$, and is written $\dim V$.

Basis Theorem#

Let $V$ be a subspace of dimension $m$. Then:

• Any $m$ linearly independent vectors in $V$ form a basis for $V$.

• Any $m$ vectors that span $V$ form a basis for $V$.

Bases as Coordinate Systems#

If $\mathcal {B} = \lbrace v_1, v_2,…, v_m\rbrace$ is a basis for a subspace $V$, then any vector $x$ in $V$ can be written as a linear combination

$x = c_1 v_1 + c_2 v_2 + ··· + c_mv_m$ in exactly one way.

Let $\mathcal {B} = \lbrace v_1, v_2,…, v_m\rbrace$ be a basis of a subspace $V$, and let

$x = c_1 v_1 + c_2 v_2 + ··· + c_mv_m$ be a vector in $V$. The coefﬁcients $c_1 , c_2 ,…, c_m$ are the coordinates of $x$ with respect to $\mathcal {B}$ . The $\mathcal {B}$-coordinate vector of $x$ is the vector

$$ [x]_{\mathcal{B}}\begin{pmatrix}c_1\\\\c_2\\\\...\\\\c_m\end{pmatrix} \quad\text{in}\quad R^m $$

Standard Basis#

  • $dim(R^n) = n$

  • $dim(P_n) = n + 1$, P is the polynomial, can be detoned as $P(x) = a_0+ a_1x+…+a_nx$

  • $dim(M_{mn})=mn$

Rank#

The rank of a matrix $A$, written rank$(A)$, is the dimension of the column space Col$(A)$.

The nullity of a matrix $A$, written $\text{nullity}(A)$, is the dimension of the null space Nul$(A)$.

If $A$ is a matrix with $n$ columns, then rank$(A)$ + nullity$(A) = n$. In other words, for any consistent system of linear equations, (dim of column span) + (dim of solution set) = (number of variables).
