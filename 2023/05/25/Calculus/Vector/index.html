Vector

Defination of Vector

A vector is a directed line segment. Given points $P$ and $Q$ (either in the plane or in space), we denote with $\vec {PQ}$ the vector from $P$ to $Q$. The point $P$ is said to be the initial point of the vector, and the point $Q$ is the terminal point.

The magnitude, length or norm of $\vec {PQ}$ is the length of the line segment $\overline {PQ}$: $|| \overline{PQ} ||$ = $||\vec {PQ} ||$ .

Two vectors are equal if they have the same magnitude and direction.

Vector Algebra

  1. Let $\vec u = ⟨ u_1 , u_2 ⟩\quad \text{and} \quad v = ⟨ v_1 , v_2 ⟩$ be vectors in $\mathbb{R}^2$ , and let $c$ be a scalar.

​ a. The addition, or sum, of the vectors $\vec u$ and $\vec v$ is the vector

​ $\vec u + \vec v = ⟨ u_1 + v_1 , u_2 + v_2⟩ $

​ b. The scalar product of $c$ and $\vec v$ is the vector

​ $c\vec v = c ⟨ v_1, v_2⟩ = ⟨ cv_1, cv_2⟩$

  1. Let $u = ⟨ u_1, u_2, u_3⟩ \quad \text{and}\quad \vec v = ⟨ v_1, v_2, v_3⟩$ be vectors in $\mathbb{R}^3$, and let $c$ be a scalar.

​ a. The addition, or sum, of the vectors $\vec u$ and $\vec v$ is the vector

​ $ \vec u+\vec v = ⟨ u_1 + v_1 , u_2 + v_2 , u_3 + v_3 ⟩ $

​ b. The scalar product of $c$ and $\vec v$ is the vector

​ $c\vec v = c ⟨ v_1 , v_2 , v_3 ⟩ = ⟨ cv_1 , cv_2 , cv_3 ⟩ $

Unit Vectors

The unit vector in the direction of a nonzero vector $\vec v$ is $ \vec u = \vec v \displaystyle \frac 1 {||\vec v ||}$

Standard Unit Vectors

  1. In $\mathbb{R}^2$, the standard unit vectors are $\vec i = ⟨ 1, 0 ⟩\quad\text{and}\quad\vec j = ⟨ 0, 1 ⟩$
  2. In $\mathbb{R}^3$, the standard unit vectors are $\vec i = ⟨ 1, 0,0 ⟩\quad\text{and}\quad\vec j = ⟨ 0, 1,0 ⟩ \quad\text{and}\quad\vec k = ⟨ 0,0, 1 ⟩$

Dot Product

  1. Let $\vec u = ⟨ u _1, u _2 ⟩$ and $\vec v = ⟨ v_1 , v_2 ⟩$ in $\mathbb{R}^2$ . The dot product of $\vec u$ and $\vec v$, denoted $\vec u\cdot \vec v$, is

$\vec u\cdot \vec v=u_1v_1+u_2v_2$

  1. Let $\vec u = ⟨ u _1, u _2,u_3 ⟩$ and $\vec v = ⟨ v_1 , v_2,v_3 ⟩$ in $\mathbb{R}^3$ . The dot product of $\vec u$ and $\vec v$, denoted $\vec u\cdot \vec v$, is

$\vec u\cdot \vec v=u_1v_1+u_2v_2+u_3v_3$

  1. Let $\vec u$ and $\vec v $ in $\mathbb{R}^2$ or $\mathbb{R}^3$. Then $\vec u\cdot \vec v=||\vec u||||\vec v||cos\theta$, where $θ$, $0 ≤ θ ≤ π$, is the angle between $\vec u$ and $\vec v$.

Orthogonal Projection

Let nonzero vectors $\vec u$ and $\vec v$ be given. The orthogonal projection of $\vec u$ onto $\vec v$, denoted $proj_{\vec v} \vec u$, is

$proj_{\vec v} \vec u=\displaystyle\frac {\vec u \vec v}{\vec v \vec v}\vec v$

$\vec u$ can be written as the sum of two vectors, one of which is parallel to $\vec v$, and one of which is orthogonal to $\vec v$, $$\vec u = \underset{\parallel \vec v}{\underbrace{proj_{\vec v} \vec u}}+\underset{\perp \vec v}{\underbrace{(\vec u - proj_{\vec v} \vec u)}}$$

Cross Product

Let $\vec u = ⟨ u_1 , u_2 , u_3 ⟩$ and $\vec v = ⟨ v_1 , v_2 , v_3 ⟩$ be vectors in $\mathbb{R}^3 $ . The cross product of $u$ and $v$, denoted $\vec u$ x $\vec v$, is the vector

$\vec u$ x $\vec v$ = $⟨ u_2 v_3 − u_3 v_2 , −(u_1 v_3 − u_3 v_1 ), u_1 v_2− u_2 v_1⟩ $

Properties of the Cross Product

$$
(\vec u \times \vec v)\cdot \vec u =0\
(\vec u \times \vec v)\cdot \vec v =0
\vec u \times \vec u = \vec 0\
\vec u \times \vec 0 = \vec 0\
\vec u \cdot (\vec v \times \vec w) = (\vec u \times \vec v) \cdot \vec w
$$

The Cross Product and Angles

Let $\vec u$ and $\vec v$ be nonzero vectors in $\mathbb{R}^3$ . Then $||\vec u \times \vec v||=||\vec u||||\vec v||sin\theta$, where $θ$, $0 ≤ θ ≤ π,$ is the angle between $\vec u$ and $\vec v$.

Equations of Lines in Space

Consider the line in space that passes through $p = ⟨ x_0 , y_0 , z_0 ⟩$ in the direction of $d = ⟨ a, b, c ⟩$ .
$$
\text{ The vector equation of the line is}\\
\vec {\ell}(t) = \vec p + t\vec d\\
\text{The parametric equations of the line are}\\
x = x_0 + at, y = y_0 + bt, z = z_0 + ct.\\
\text{ The symmetric equations of the line are}\\
\frac {x−x_0} a = \frac{y−y_0} b = \frac{z-z_0} c
$$

Equations of a Plane in Standard and General Forms

The plane passing through the point $P = (x_0 , y_0 , z_0 )$ with normal vector (that is orthogonal to the plane.) $\vec n = ⟨ a, b, c ⟩$ can be described by an equation with standard form

$a(x − x_0 ) + b(y − y_0 ) + c(z − z_0 ) = 0$;

the equation’s general form is

$ax + by + cz = d$.

Distance from a Point to a Plane

Let a plane with normal vector $\vec n$ be given, and let $Q$ be a point. The distance $h$ from $Q$ to the plane is $h= \displaystyle\frac {|\vec n \vec {PQ} |}{||\vec n||}$

Vector–Valued Functions

A vector-valued function is a function of the form

$\vec r (t) = \langle f(t),g(t)\rangle \quad \text{or} \quad \vec r(t)= \langle f(t),g(t),h(t) \rangle $

where $f, g$ and $h$ are real valued functions. The domain of $\vec r$ is the set of all values of t for which $\vec r(t)$ is deﬁned. The range of $\vec r$ is the set of all possible output vector $\vec r(t)$.

Operations on Vector–Valued Functions

Let $\vec r_1 (t) = \langle f_1(t),g_1(t)\rangle$ and $\vec r_2 (t) = \langle f_2(t),g_2(t)\rangle$ be vector–valued functions in $\mathbb{R} ^2$ and let c be a scalar. Then:
$$
\vec r_1 (t) \pm r_2(t) = \langle f_1(t)\pm f_2(t),g_1(t)\pm g_2(t)\rangle\\
\vec r_1(t) = \langle cf_1(t),cg_1(t)\rangle
$$
A similar deﬁnition holds for vector–valued functions in $\mathbb{R}^3$ .

Average Rate of Change

Let $\vec r(t)$ be a vector–valued function, where each of its component functions is continuous on its domain, and let $t_0 < t_1$ . The average rate of change of $\vec r(t)$ on $[t_0 , t_1 ]$ is

average rate of change = $\displaystyle \frac {\vec r (t_1)-\vec r(t_0)}{t_1-t_0}$

Limits of Vector–Valued Functions

Let I be an open interval containing $c$, and let $\vec r(t)$ be a vector–valued function deﬁned on $I$, except possibly at $c$. The limit of $ \vec r(t)$, as $t$ approaches $c$, is $L$, expressed as $\displaystyle\lim_{t \to c}{\vec r(t)}= \vec L$ means that given any $\epsilon > 0$, there exists a $δ > 0$ such that for all $t \neq c$, if $| t − c | < δ$, we have $||\vec r(t) − L || < \epsilon$.

Limits of Vector–Valued Functions

Let $\vec r(t) = ⟨ f(t), g(t) ⟩$ be a vector–valued function in $\mathbb{R}^2$ deﬁned on an open interval $I$ containing $c$, except possibly at $c$. Then
$$
\begin{align*}
\lim_{t \to c}\vec r(t) &= \langle \lim_{t \to c} f(t), \lim_{t \to c} g(t)\rangle\\
\vec r’(t) &= \lim_{h \to 0} \frac {\vec r(t+h)-\vec r(t)} {h} \\
\vec r’(t) &= \langle f’(t), g’(t) \rangle
\end{align*}
$$

Function of Two Variables

Let $D$ be a subset of $\mathbb{R}^2$ . A function f of two variables is a rule that assigns each pair $(x, y)$ in $D$ a value $z = f(x, y)$ in $\mathbb{R}$. $D$ is the domain of $f$; the set of all outputs of f is the range.

Limit of a Function of Two Variables

Let $S$ be a set containing $P = (x_0 , y_0 )$ where every open disk centered at $P$ contains points in $S$ other than $P$, let $f$ be a function of two variables deﬁned on $S$, except possibly at $P$, and let $L$ be a real number. The limit of $f(x, y)$ as $(x, y)$ approaches $(x_0 , y_0 )$ is $L$, denoted
$$
\lim_{(x,y)\to(x_0,y_0)}f(x,y) = L
$$
means that given any $\epsilon > 0$, there exists $\delta > 0$ such that for all $(x, y)$ in $S$, where $(x, y) \neq (x_0 , y_0 )$, if $(x, y)$ is in the open disk centered at $(x_0 , y_0 )$ with radius $\delta$, then $| f(x, y) − L | < \epsilon$.

Basic Limit Properties of Functions of Two Variables

Let b, $x_0$, $y_0$, $L$, and $K$ be real numbers, let $n$ be a positive integer, and let $f$ and $g$ be functions with the following limits: $\lim_{(x,y)\rightarrow (x_0,y_0)} f(x,y) = L$, $\lim_{(x,y)\rightarrow (x_0,y_0)} g(x,y)=K$

Constants:

$\displaystyle\lim_{(x,y)\rightarrow (x_0,y_0)} c = c$, where $c$ is a constant.

Identity:

$\displaystyle\lim_{(x,y)\rightarrow (x_0,y_0)} x = x_0,\displaystyle\lim_{(x,y)\rightarrow (x_0,y_0)} y = y_0$.

Sums/Differences:

$\displaystyle\lim_{(x,y)\rightarrow (x_0,y_0)} [f(x,y) \pm g(x,y)] = L \pm K$.

Scalar Multiples:

$\displaystyle\lim_{(x,y)\rightarrow (x_0,y_0)} [cf(x,y)] = cL$, where $c$ is a constant.

Products:

$\displaystyle\lim_{(x,y)\rightarrow (x_0,y_0)} [f(x,y) \cdot g(x,y)] = L \cdot K$.

Quotients:

$\displaystyle\lim_{(x,y)\rightarrow (x_0,y_0)} \frac{f(x,y)}{g(x,y)}= \frac{L}{K}$, provided that $K\neq 0$.

Powers:

$\displaystyle\lim_{(x,y)\rightarrow (x_0,y_0)} [f(x,y)]^n = L^n$.

Continuous

Let a function $f(x, y)$ be deﬁned on a set $S$ containing the point $(x_0 , y_0 )$.

  1. $f$ is continuous at $(x_0,y_0)$ if $\displaystyle\lim_{(x,y)\to(x_0,y_0)}f(x,y)=f(x_0,y_0)$

  2. $f$ is continuous on $S$ if f is continuous at all points in S. If f is continuous at all points in $\mathbb{R}^2$ , we say that f is continuous everywhere.

Partial Derivative

Let $z = f(x, y)$ be a continuous function on a set $S$ in $\mathbb{R}^2$ .

  1. The partial derivative of $f$ with respect to $x$ is:

$$
f_x(x,y)= \lim_{h\to0}\frac{f(x+h,y)-f(x,y)}h
$$

  1. The partial derivative of $f$ with respect to $y$ is:
    $$
    f_y(x,y)= \lim_{h\to0}\frac{f(x,y+h)-f(x,y)}h
    $$

Second Partial Derivative, Mixed Partial Derivative

Let $z = f(x, y)$ be continuous on a set $S$.

  1. The second partial derivative of f with respect to $x$ then $x$ is

$\frac {\partial} {\partial x} (\frac {\partial f} {\partial x})=(\frac {\partial^2 f} {\partial x \partial x})={(f_x)}_ x = f_{xx}$

  1. The second partial derivative of f with respect to $x$ then $y$ is

$\frac {\partial} {\partial y} (\frac {\partial f} {\partial x})=(\frac {\partial^2 f} {\partial x \partial y})={(f_x)}_ y=f_{xy}$

Mixed Partial Derivatives

Let $f$ be deﬁned such that $f_{xy}$ and $f_{yx}$ are continuous on a set $S$. Then for each point $(x, y)$ in $S$, $f_{xy} (x, y) = f_{yx}(x, y)$.

Total Diﬀerential

Let $z = f(x, y)$ be continuous on a set $S$. Let $dx$ and $dy$ represent changes in $x$ and $y$, respectively. Where the partial derivatives $f_x$ and $f_y$ exist, the total diﬀerential of $z$ is $dz = f_x(x,y)dx+f_y(x,y)dy$

Multivariable Diﬀerentiability

Let $z = f(x, y)$ be deﬁned on a set $S$ containing $(x_0 , y_0 )$ where $f_x (x_0 , y_0 )$ and $f_y (x_0 , y_0 )$ exist. Let $dz$ be the total diﬀerential of $z$ at $(x_0 , y _0)$, let $\Delta z = f(x_0 + dx, y_0 + dy) − f(x_0 , y_0 )$, and let $E_x$ and $E_y$ be functions of $dx$ and $dy$ such that $\Delta z = dz+E_xdx+E_ydy $

  1. We say $f$ is diﬀerentiable at $(x_0, y_0)$ if, given $\epsilon > 0$, there is a $\delta > 0$ such that if $|| ⟨ dx, dy ⟩ || $< δ, then $|| ⟨ E_x , E_y ⟩ || < \epsilon$. That is, as $dx$ and $dy$ go to 0, so do $E_x$ and $E_y$ .

  2. We say $f$ is diﬀerentiable on $S$ if f is diﬀerentiable at every point in $S$. If f is diﬀerentiable on $\mathbb{R}^2$ , we say that f is diﬀerentiable everywhere.

Multivariable Chain Rule

Suppose $z = f(x,y)$ and $x = g(t)$ and $y = h(t)$. Then, the chain rule for the partial derivative of $z$ with respect to $t$ is:

$\frac{\partial z}{\partial t} = \frac{\partial z}{\partial x} \frac{\partial x}{\partial t} + \frac{\partial z}{\partial y} \frac{\partial y}{\partial t}=\langle f_x,f_y \rangle \cdot \langle x’,y’ \rangle$

Implicit Diﬀerentiation

Let $f$ be a diﬀerentiable function of $x$ and $y$, where $f(x, y) = c$ deﬁnes $y$ as an implicit function of $x$, for some constant $c$. Then $ \frac {dy} {dx} = - \frac {f_x(x,y)}{f_y(x,y)}$

Directional Derivatives

Let $z = f(x, y)$ be continuous on a set $S$ and let $\vec u = ⟨ u_1 , u_2 ⟩$ be a unit vector. For all points $(x, y$), the directional derivative of $f$ at $(x, y)$ in the direction of $\vec u$ is
$$
D_{\vec u}f(x,y = \lim_{h \to 0} \frac {f(x+hu_1,y+hu_2)-f(x,y)} h=f_x(x_0,y_0)u_1+f_y(x_0,y_0)u_2
$$

Gradient

Let $z = f(x, y)$ be diﬀerentiable on a set $S$ that contains the point $(x_0 , y_0 )$.

  1. The gradient of $f$ is $\nabla (x, y) = ⟨f_x (x, y), f_y (x, y)⟩$ .

  2. The gradient of $f$ at $(x_0 , y_0)$ is $\nabla f(x_0 , y_0 )$ =$⟨ f _x (x _0 , y_0 ), f _y (x _0, y_0 )⟩$ .

so the directional derivative of $f$ can be rewritten as $D_{\vec u }f = \nabla f \cdot \vec u$

The Gradient and Directional Derivatives

Let $z = f(x, y)$ be diﬀerentiable on a set $S$ with gradient $\nabla f$, let $P = (x_0 , y_0 )$ be a point in $S$ and let $u$ be a unit vector.

  1. The maximum value of $D_{\vec u} f(x_0 , y _0)$ is $|| \nabla f(x_0 , y_0 ) ||$ ; the direction of maximal $z$ increase is $ \nabla f(x_0 , y_0 )$.

  2. The minimum value of $D_{\vec u} f(x_0 , y _0)$) is $-|| \nabla f(x_0 , y_0 ) ||$; the direction of minimal $z $ increase is $- \nabla f(x_0 , y_0 )$..

  3. At $P$, $\nabla f(x_0 , y_0 )$. is orthogonal to the level curve passing through $( x_0 , y_0 , f(x_0 , y_0 ) )$ .

Directional Tangent Line

Let $z = f(x, y)$ be diﬀerentiable on a set $S$ containing $(x _0 , y_0 )$ and let$ \vec u = ⟨ u_1 , u_2 ⟩$ be a unit vector.

$l_x(t) = \begin{cases}
x = x_0 + at \
y = y_0 + bt \
z = f(x_0 + at, y_0 + bt)
\end{cases}$

$l_y(t) = \begin{cases}
x = x_0 \
y = y_0 + bt \
z = f(x_0, y_0 + bt)
\end{cases}$

$l_{\mathbf{u}}(t) = \begin{cases}
x = x_0 + at \
y = y_0 + bt \
z = f(x_0 + at, y_0 + bt)
\end{cases}$

Tangent Plane

Let $z = f(x,y)$ be differentiable on a set $S$ containing $(x_0, y_0)$, where $a = f_x(x_0, y_0)$, $b = f_y(x_0, y_0)$, $\vec n = \langle a, b, -1 \rangle$, and $P = (x_0, y_0, f(x_0,y_0))$. The plane through $P$ with normal vector $\vec n$ is the tangent plane to $f$ at $P$. The standard form of this plane is:

$a(x - x_0) + b(y - y_0) - (z - f(x_0, y_0)) = 0$
